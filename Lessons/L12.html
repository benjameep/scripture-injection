Introduction

The Book of Mormon was written to convince everyone, both Jews and Gentiles, that "Jesus is the Christ, the Eternal God, manifesting himself unto all nations" (title page). Missionary work is therefore a central theme and one of the divine purposes of the Book of Mormon. Elder David A.

Bednar explained that  "the Book of Mormon in combination with the Spirit of the Lord is ‘the greatest single tool which God has given us to convert the world.’ This Restoration volume of scripture is the keystone of our religion and is essential in bringing souls to the Savior." (Missionary, Family History, and Temple Work, Ensign, Oct. 2014).

Today there are more missionaries of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints than there have been at any time in our history. The Book of Mormon was uniquely prepared to help members and missionaries to teach the gospel with power and authority and to bring souls unto Christ. Throughout its pages, the Book of Mormon shows the challenges and joys of sharing the gospel, as well as the importance of what and how to teach.

We live in the long prophesied day when the gospel of Jesus Christ is being "declared to every nation, kindred, tongue, and people" (Mosiah 15:28). As we study the Book of Mormon, we can learn how to be effective instruments in the hands of the Lord in inviting all to come unto Christ and to live His gospel.

The Scattering and Gathering of Israel

Introduction

The Book of Mormon was written primarily to "the remnant of the House of Israel," so that they could “know the covenants of the Lord, that they are not cast off forever” (title page). The Book of Mormon is the instrument God has prepared to bring about the promised gathering of Israel. Elder Russell M. Nelson taught that "the Book of Mormon declares the advent of the gathering and is God’s instrument to bring about that gathering. Without the Book of Mormon, there would be no gathering of Israel." (The Book of Mormon, the Gathering of Israel, and the Second Coming, Ensign, July 2014). 

So, when and why was Israel scattered? Throughout Israel’s history they have been scattered as they have rejected the Lord and his prophets and have broken their covenants. The Book of Mormon discusses three of the most significant scatterings which Israel has experienced in its history.

First, the northern kingdom of Israel, consisting most of the ten tribes, was scattered by the Assyrians in 722 B.C., and they have become known as the lost ten tribes.

Second, the southern kingdom of Judah and its capitol city of Jerusalem were destroyed by the Babylonians in 587 B.C., and many Jews were carried captive into Babylon. Fifty years later, after Babylon was overrun by the Persians, King Cyrus permitted some of the Jews to return to Jerusalem and rebuild their temple.

The final significant scattering of Israel occurred in 70 A.D. when Jerusalem was destroyed by the Romans as the Savior had foretold during his ministry. 

Why is the scattering of Israel significant for us today? The Book of Mormon teaches that although Israel was scattered, the Lord would preserve his covenant people, and gather them again in the latter days. For anyone who has felt forgotten, left behind, or abandoned, the prophesies of the Book of Mormon concerning the gathering of Israel teach that God has never forgotten His people. Today the Lord is working in mighty power to gather scattered Israel to the knowledge of Jesus Christ, to a knowledge of the covenants of the Lord, and to the priesthood power and saving ordinances restored to the earth and found only within the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints.

We are part of the remnant of scattered Israel that the Book of Mormon was written to. One by one, the Lord is gathering His people to His gospel and His church. We are privileged to live in a day when the Lord is gathering Israel, both living and dead, through our missionary efforts and temple work. This is part of the fulfillment that through the posterity of Abraham all the families of the earth would be blessed.
